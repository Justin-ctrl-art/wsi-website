Stories

The resilience of survivors.
Told with care.

We tell stories slowly. With consent. With the survivor as co-author. What follows are not accounts of suffering for its own sake — they are testimonies to what healing can look like, when it is given time and the right kind of attention.

Editorial Note

Every story on this page is published with full, written consent. Names and identifying details may be changed to protect the storyteller and those they love. Our goal is to honor dignity — not to illustrate suffering. Storytellers may withdraw at any time, and we will remove their words without question.

Featured Story

Veteran · Reintegration

"The war ended. Coming home took longer."

For years I thought the quiet was the problem. What I needed was someone patient enough to sit inside it with me.

Eli came to us four years after his last deployment. He had a steady job, a home, a daughter in second grade — and he could not sleep for more than three hours at a stretch. He arrived quiet, unsure whether he belonged in a room of survivors.

"I didn't lose anyone. I wasn't injured. I thought I had no right to ask for help." What followed was eighteen months of integrated care — trauma-focused therapy, peer community, and time with a clinician who did not rush him. The progress was not linear. It rarely is.

Today, Eli sleeps through the night most nights. He is still careful with loud rooms. He talks, sometimes, to newer veterans who arrive where he once was — unsure whether their pain counts. He tells them it does.

As told to Maya Okonkwo · March 2026
