About Us

A mission forged in the aftermath of war.

For more than two decades, we have sat with the people war leaves behind — listening, studying, and building the long, patient work of recovery. This is who we are, and why we do it.

Our Mission

What we are here to do.

The War Survivors Institute restores whole health to those harmed by war, conducts projects to promote mutuality and prevent violent conflict, and provides education and training.

Whole health is not a slogan. It is a clinical commitment — to body, mind, relationships, and meaning — that guides how we treat trauma, how we design our programs, and how we measure what we do. It is the reason our work reaches beyond therapy hours into the quieter, longer labor of helping a person feel at home in the world again.

And because no clinician can hold a person whole alone, we also build the conditions that make healing possible: training for providers, peer community for survivors, and the research that keeps our practice honest.

Our Story

A quiet founding, a long arc.

The Institute began in 2004, in a modest clinic in Albuquerque, with a single, stubborn question: why were so many of the refugees and veterans Dr. Michael Hollifield saw in his practice slipping through every screening, every program, every safety net built to catch them? The answer, he came to believe, was that most systems were built to detect illness — not to recognize survival.

From that question grew a slow, deliberate institution. In its first years, the Institute's work was clinical and research-driven: validating the Refugee Health Screener-15, publishing in peer-reviewed journals, and partnering with a handful of local resettlement agencies. Funding was small, the staff smaller. But the cases taught the model, and the model, in turn, taught the work.

By the 2010s, the Institute had begun training clinicians nationally and extending its reach abroad — first to Sweden, then to Uganda, then to fifteen countries and counting. Each partnership brought a new language, a new war, and a new lesson in how the same human wound is shaped by culture, circumstance, and time.

Today, the Institute holds close to its founding conviction: that survivors deserve care that is evidence-based and unhurried, clinical and humane. We are not a crisis service. We are a place where, with time, people learn to carry what they have been through — and still find a life they can call their own.

Our Values

Four commitments that shape our work.

These are not aspirations. They are the standards we hold each other — and ourselves — to, in every session, every study, and every conversation.

01

Wholeness

We treat the whole person — body, mind, relationships, meaning — not a diagnosis in isolation. Healing that honors only part of a life cannot hold.

02

Mutuality

Clinicians, survivors, and communities know things about recovery that no single group holds alone. We build the work together, and share authorship for what we learn.

03

Evidence

We study what we do, publish what we find, and change course when the data asks us to. Compassion without rigor is a kind of carelessness.

04

Dignity

We do not sensationalize suffering. We do not collect stories. We show up with time, consent, and the care any person deserves.

Michael Hollifield, M.D.

Founder, President & CEO

Leadership · Founder

A physician's long attention.

Dr. Michael Hollifield is a board-certified psychiatrist whose clinical and research career has centered, for more than thirty years, on the mental health of refugees and survivors of organized violence. He has practiced in academic medical centers and community clinics across the United States, and has consulted on refugee mental health in Europe, East Africa, and Southeast Asia.

He is best known as the principal developer of the Refugee Health Screener-15 (RHS-15), a brief, culturally adapted instrument now used in primary care and resettlement settings across more than a dozen languages. The tool grew out of a recognition that standard screeners were missing the very patients they were meant to help, and that a better instrument could only be built in partnership with the communities it served.

Dr. Hollifield founded the War Survivors Institute in 2004 to bring his clinical practice, research, and training under a single institutional roof — one patient enough to do careful work, and rigorous enough to keep itself honest. He continues to see patients weekly, teach at the Institute's fellowship, and advise ministries of health on refugee mental health policy.
